# SOL Lens three-minute demo narration

Target length: **2:50–3:00** at a calm speaking pace. Record the browser at 1080p or higher, keep the pointer movements deliberate, and pause briefly after each verdict appears.

## 0:00–0:20 — The problem

**On screen:** Open `https://sol-lens.onrender.com/`. Hold on the headline and the reference-agent versus candidate-agent cards.

**Narration:**

> When teams migrate an agent, they often judge the change from final-answer vibes. That hides whether the workflow gained evidence, preserved its constraints, or introduced contradictions. SOL Lens turns observable agent activity into an inspectable semantic comparison and asks a more useful question: did the candidate agent actually improve on the reference agent?

## 0:20–0:42 — What SOL means

**On screen:** Point to the identity strip and the two repository links in the title bar.

**Narration:**

> Here, SOL means Self-Organizing Logos. It is the research framework behind the project, not the GPT-5.6 Sol model name. The SOL Engine is the experimental and mathematical foundation. SOL Lens is the browser workbench that applies a smaller deterministic profile to observable traces. Both source repositories are linked directly in the title bar.

## 0:42–1:08 — The curated examples

**On screen:** Select **Explore 7 examples**. Slowly move across Tour 1, Tour 2, and Tour 3.

**Narration:**

> Judges do not need to create JSON or understand Logons before trying the product. Seven built-in packets cover linear chains, branching evidence, parallel tool use, self-correction, multi-agent handoffs, conflicting sources, and grouped program-scale work. The suggested tour moves from a simple grounded answer, to a tool fan-out, to a failure case with conflicting evidence.

## 1:08–1:35 — Reading a Logon graph

**On screen:** Load **Grounded answer**. Point to two or three nodes and their connecting edges, then the evidence, coherence, and contradiction cards.

**Narration:**

> Each Logon is one observable atomic unit: a request, tool call, result, check, contradiction, or output. Typed edges show how those units support, constrain, or challenge one another. SOL Lens replays the packet locally and scores three things: evidence quality, semantic coherence, and unresolved contradiction. The court then applies explicit gates to return PROMOTE, HOLD, or QUARANTINE.

## 1:35–1:58 — A bounded HOLD

**On screen:** Open the gallery, load **Parallel tool fan-out**, and point to the reference/candidate delta and HOLD verdict.

**Narration:**

> This twenty-four-Logon example dispatches six parallel tool branches. Most evidence is useful, but one security constraint remains unresolved. The result is HOLD—not a vague warning, but a replayable decision tied to observable fields. A reviewer can select any node, filter by status, and inspect the exact source and pressure values behind the graph.

## 1:58–2:25 — A visible failure

**On screen:** Load **Conflicting sources**. Show the 120-Logon graph, then scroll to the comparison cards and QUARANTINE verdict.

**Narration:**

> Now the conflicting-sources packet compares six evidence branches across one hundred twenty Logons. Contradiction rises from point zero nine in the reference trace to point two four in the candidate. The court moves from PROMOTE to QUARANTINE. This is the central value of SOL Lens: a candidate can look productive while its semantic evidence structure clearly says it is not ready.

## 2:25–2:42 — Replayable proof

**On screen:** Point to **Download v0.2 proof packet**. Download it if the recording flow permits, then return to the verdict.

**Narration:**

> Every verdict can be exported as a versioned proof packet. It contains the observable Logons, typed edges, metrics, and recomputed court result. There are no hidden-chain-of-thought claims, and the same packet can be independently validated and replayed.

## 2:42–3:00 — Foundation and close

**On screen:** Return to the title bar and point to the SOL Engine and SOL Lens repository links. End on the full dashboard.

**Narration:**

> SOL Lens was built during OpenAI Build Week with GPT-5.6 and Codex, on top of the pre-existing SOL research program. The repositories link to the mathematics, experiments, tests, provenance, and application source. SOL Lens makes agent migration evidence visible, deterministic, and reviewable before a workflow reaches production.

## Recording checklist

- Keep the final export click optional if a browser download prompt would interrupt the recording.
- Do not linger on every example; the three marked tour stops tell the complete story.
- Keep the conflict verdict visible for at least three seconds.
- Use the phrase **Self-Organizing Logos** once, and explicitly distinguish it from the model name.
- End on the live URL or repository links so judges know where to continue exploring.
